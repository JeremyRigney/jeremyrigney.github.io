Research


The LOw Frequency ARray Telescope (LOFAR)

LOFAR is a the largest low frequency radio telescope in the world, with 52 stations located throughout Europe. It observes the univers from 10 to 270 MHz. It utilises interferometry to combine it's 52 stations to act as one large radio dish, with a diameter equivalent to 2000 kilometers. My research with LOFAR focuses on searching for radio emission from low mass active stars such as M dwarfs. Many exoplanets have been detected around these stars due to their sutability for detection via the transit method. However, their stellar environment is not well understood, and we do not yet know if these closely orbiting exoplanets are constantly bombarded by high energy stellar flares and coronal mass ejections. Observing these stars at low frequencies helps us to determine the emission mechanisms powering stellar activity, and whether these stars have magnetic fields high enough to contain their plasma outbursts that would otherwise destroy any possibility of life forming on other planets.

Australian Square Kilometre Array Precursor Telescope (ASKAP)

ASKAP is located in the Murchison Radio Quiet Zone in Western Australia. It is comprised of 36 13 meter dishes observing at frequencies between 700 MHz to 1.4 GHz. The signals from the dishes can be combined to create one large telescope with a baseline of 6 kilometeres and arcsecond resolution. ASKAP is primarily a survey telescope with a wide 36 square degree field of view. This makes it ideal for searching for M dwarfs with radio emission in a large region of the sky at once. My research using ASKAP focused on searching for M dwarfs in two fields which were co-observed with the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS). This spacecraft provided high cadence optical data to determine when flares occured from any stars in the field. The results of this research have been written in a paper currently in review.

M Dwarf Stars

M dwarfs are low mass stars (0.075 - 0.6 times the mass of the Sun) which make up about 70 percent of the stellar population They can just about sustain hydrogen fusion and have highly convective cores, often coupled with rapid rotation rates. This can generate large magnetic dynamos which sustain kilogauss magnetic fields throughout the star. As a result M dwarfs are very active, with magnetic reconnection producing flares tens of thousands of times larger than those observed from the Sun.
