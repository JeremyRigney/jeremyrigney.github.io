01 — What Is It

A geometric alignment of Sun, Moon, and Earth

A solar eclipse happens when the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun. In a partial eclipse, the Moon does not completely cover the Sun from your location, so a crescent of sunlight remains visible. The exact shape depends on where you are standing and the local timing of the alignment. No two vantage points see quite the same event.

02 — When It Happens

Late afternoon to early evening over Ireland

On August 12th, from Dublin, the partial eclipse runs from first contact at 18:13 to final contact at 20:05 IST, with maximum eclipse at 19:11. The Sun will be sitting low to the west-north-west, so a clear horizon matters.

04 — Path of Totality

Where the Sun goes fully dark

Ireland only ever sees a partial eclipse. The narrow track of totality, where the Moon blots the Sun out completely, sweeps down out of the Arctic, crosses western Iceland, races over the North Atlantic and makes landfall in northern Spain around sunset. To stand in the Moon's full shadow, that band is where you would need to be.

05 — Eye Safety

Never view the Sun directly without proper protection

  • Use certified solar viewers that meet ISO 12312-2.
  • Do not use normal sunglasses, camera filters, or smoked glass.
  • Make a pinhole camera to project the eclipse onto a surface.
  • If using telescopes or binoculars, use approved front-mounted solar filters.
  • Supervise children closely during observation.
